 classical many-body problem at the atomistic level
    In programming, the classical many-body problem refers to the computational challenge of simulating the behavior of a system consisting of a large number of interacting particles or bodies according to classical mechanics. Each particle interacts with every other particle in the system through forces such as gravity, electromagnetic forces, or other potential functions. This problem arises in various fields such as physics, chemistry, biology, and materials science, where understanding the collective behavior of many interacting entities is essential. 
        Some common examples include 
                molecular dynamics simulations,
                astrophysical N-body simulations,
                simulations of granular materials.
    To tackle this problem, various algorithms and techniques have been developed, including:

        Particle-based methods: These simulate the behavior of individual particles over time, updating their positions and velocities based on the forces acting on them. Examples include molecular dynamics methods like Verlet integration and neighbor-list algorithms.        VERLET INTEGRATION, NEIGHBOR LIST ALGORITHM

        Grid-based methods: These divide the simulation space into a grid and calculate interactions between particles within neighboring grid cells. This reduces the number of pairwise interactions that need to be computed, improving efficiency. Examples include Barnes-Hut tree algorithms for gravitational N-body simulations.    BARNES-HUT TREE ALGORITHMS(GRAVITATIONAL N-BODY SIMULATION)

        Fast multipole methods (FMM): FMM approximates the interactions between distant particles by recursively subdividing the simulation space into larger and smaller regions, allowing for efficient calculation of long-range interactions.  FAST MULTIPOLE METHODS(FMM)

Rigid molecules require the use of the Euler equations, perhaps expressed in terms of Hamilton’s quaternions.
Molecules with internal degrees of freedom, but that are also subject to structural constraints, might involve the Lagrange method for incorporating geometric constraints into the dynamical equations.

Normal equilibrium MD corresponds to the microcanon- ical ensemble of statistical mechanics, but in certain cases properties at constant temperature (and sometimes pressure) are required; there are ways of modifying the equations of motion to produce such systems, but of course the individual tra- jectories no longer represent the solution of Newton’s equations.


Termin "potencjał Lennarda-Jonesa" odnosi się do potencjału opisującego oddziaływania międzyatomowe, które występują w wielu modelach cząsteczkowych. Ma on charakterystyczne dwie części: przyciągającą, która jest proporcjonalna do odwrotności szóstej potęgi odległości między atomami, oraz odpychającą, która szybko wzrasta, gdy odległość między atomami maleje do zera. To właśnie silnie odpychające jądro tego potencjału powoduje niestabilność trajektorii atomów, co oznacza, że nawet najmniejsze zakłócenie może wywołać znaczne zmiany w zachowaniu atomów w czasie.

Ogólnie rzecz biorąc, zdanie to sugeruje, że ze względu na tę niestabilność, próba uzyskania bardzo dokładnych trajektorii atomowych jest trudna, a nawet niemożliwa w praktyce, szczególnie w dłuższych okresach czasu. Dlatego też, w symulacjach dynamiki molekularnej czy innych modelach cząsteczkowych, często akceptuje się pewien stopień niedokładności w trajektoriach atomów, skupiając się raczej na ogólnych trendach i zjawiskach zachodzących w systemie. Liquids represent the state of matter most frequently studied by MD methods.

Possible applications

Order of phase transitions


Fundamental studies: equilibration, tests of molecular chaos, kinetic theory, diffusion, transport properties, size dependence, tests of models and potential functions.

Phase transitions: first- and second-order, phase coexistence, order parameters, critical phenomena.

Collective behavior: decay of space and time correlation functions, coupling of translational and rotational motion, vibration, spectroscopic measurements, orientational order, dielectric properties.

Complex fluids: structure and dynamics of glasses, molecular liquids, pure wa- ter and aqueous solutions, liquid crystals, ionic liquids, fluid interfaces, films and monolayers.

Polymers: chains, rings and branched molecules, equilibrium conformation, relaxation and transport processes.

Solids: defect formation and migration, fracture, grain boundaries, structural transformations, radiation damage, elastic and plastic mechanical properties, friction, shock waves, molecular crystals, epitaxial growth.

Biomolecules: structure and dynamics of proteins, protein folding, micelles, membranes, docking of molecules.
Fluid dynamics: laminar flow, boundary layers, rheology of non-Newtonian fluids, unstable flow.

Examples of this classification include whether the interactions are short- or long-ranged; whether the system is thermally and mechanically isolated or open to outside influence; whether, if in equilibrium, normal dynamical laws are used or the equations of motion are modified to produce a particular statistical mechanical ensemble; whether the constituent particles are simple structureless atoms or more complex molecules and, if the latter, whether the molecules are rigid or flexible; whether simple interactions are represented by continuous potential functions or by step potentials; whether interactions involve just pairs of particles or multiparticle contributions as well
